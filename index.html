Festival Data: Defining Categories and Structuring the Process


In this entry, I explain how we structured the process for collecting festival data to complement audience insights in the internationalisation section. I helped define clear categories for local, national, and international artists, ensuring consistent data collection across events, and contributed to decisions that focused on where artists were based and working rather than just their origin. - Summary powered by AI

As we finalised the survey and began shaping the interviews, we planned how we’d collect and organise festival data. We knew the internationalisation section needed to go beyond audience opinion, we also needed to show how festivals were actually booking artists. The challenge was to create a data set that could meaningfully compare local, national, and international artist presence across different events and years.

We began by outlining how to collect the data accurately. After checking in with Andrew for Cheltenham Jazz Festival, we used their official brochures and the Around Town guide to make sure we were capturing both the ticketed artists and those playing free events on the different stages around the town. For 2000 Trees, I relied on ClashFinder, which had been promoted by the festival as the best way to plan your weekend across the years.

Before collecting any names, we agreed on how we would define local, national, and international artists. I suggested a two-stage process, first collect the line-ups, then research where each artist is based. Artists based in Gloucestershire were classed as local. Artists based in the UK but outside Gloucestershire were national. Artists based outside the UK and flying in for the event were international. There were edge cases we needed to consider and prepare for which I found we did well. For example, Mimi Barks, originally from Berlin but now based in London, was classed as national. We made a clear decision that being “international” meant being currently based outside the UK and travelling into the country as a deciding factor. The point was to focus on where the artist was currently working and how available they were to a UK audience, not just where they were born.

Festival Data: Challenges and Pivot


In this entry, I describe how I took the lead on rebuilding the Cheltenham Jazz Festival dataset after spotting major inconsistencies, ensuring the data met our original guidelines. I also refined our method for collecting 2000 Trees lineups, evolving my approach to include cross-checks with social media to improve accuracy and maintain the integrity of the internationalisation analysis. - Summary powered by AI

As the project progressed, it became clear that a significant amount of the group’s Cheltenham Jazz Festival data had been collected inconsistently with our guidelines. Artists had been recorded multiple times, some weren’t artists at all, and the national/international categorisations hadn’t been applied properly. I couldn’t pinpoint exactly what was wrong, but there were enough inconsistencies across entries that I decided to restart the Jazz Festival data process from scratch to make sure what we included in the report was accurate and usable.

Owen had understandably shifted his focus to the interview section, so I took on rebuilding the Jazz Festival dataset and collecting the 2000 Trees data. Owen took the lead on the survey data analysis and interviews, with some input and feedback from me to balance the workload. While the situation wasn’t ideal, I was okay with adapting my role. I was already invested in the structure, and I wanted to make sure the section delivered something solid which it couldn’t do with wildly incorrect data.

I also changed the original method we set out to use for collecting 2000 Trees festival lineups, having set out to use tour posters. This is a great example of how my thinking evolved across the project. Not just focusing on getting a list of festival lineups, I wanted to accurately collect the data to establish a solid foundation for the analysis. Another example of this evolution was once I’d collected the core data, I thought about cross-checking it, and decided to check the festival’s social media posts to catch any late changes or updates. This did impact the results as several artists withdrew at the last minute. While confirming artist locations, I also checked their social media activity around the time of the festival. This helped me rule out two or three artists who had cancelled just before their set. However, most cancelled artists were replaced, with the overall totals for national and international artists likely balancing out.

Using Tools & Methods


In this entry, I explain how I used a mix of official sources, third-party tools like ClashFinder, and social media to ensure accurate festival data collection. I also used AI as a secondary check for consistency, helping me maintain a clean, reliable, and well-structured contribution that aligned with the overall tone and quality of the report. - Summary powered by AI

Throughout the project, I relied on a combination of practical research tools, software, and industry reading to support the accuracy and direction of the section.

I used a mix of official sources and third-party tools for the data collection. ClashFinder was essential for 2000 Trees, especially as it had been promoted by the festival as the best way to navigate line-ups. For Cheltenham Jazz Festival, I used the official brochure and the Around Town guide to gather the full range of performances. Where necessary, I cross-checked artist appearances using social media activity, either through the artists’ own posts or updates from the festivals. This helped confirm cancellations or changes that weren’t reflected elsewhere.

Once I’d collected and organised the data, I ran it through AI software (ChatGPT) as a secondary check. I asked it to scan for any missing artists from ClashFinder and any inconsistencies in artist labelling. For example, if I’d marked someone as international in one year and national in another. I also used it to see if I’d missed any obvious trends in the data or any points that might stand out in analysis. It didn’t pick up anything significant and simply reinforced what I’d already observed, but it was a useful checkpoint especially given the amount of data being handled.

As a project group, we regularly checked over the progress of the report and made amendments as we progressed. I focused on keeping the festival data section clean, structured, and aligned with the wider report. Between us we made sure the tone and framing stayed consistent across the internationalisation section. The tools and sources I used in this project helped ensure my contribution was accurate, reliable, and met industry standards.

Festival Data: Analysis and Insight Writing


In this entry, I detail how I analysed the festival data using pivot tables to identify key trends, such as the growth of 2000 Trees and post-pandemic shifts at Cheltenham Jazz Festival. I focused on producing clear, evidence-based insights that aligned with the report’s professional tone, and worked seamlessly with Owen’s writing to create a cohesive, high-quality section. - Summary powered by AI

Once I was confident in the data, I moved into writing the analysis. I skipped the introduction and began noting down trends and patterns as they emerged while reviewing the data. I used pivot tables to break the data down, which made it easier to compare totals across local, national, and international artists. These were later used in the report.

From there, I started stepping back to better see what stood out. One key trend was the consistent growth of 2000 Trees, particularly in national and international acts. In contrast, Cheltenham Jazz Festival saw more fluctuation, local artist numbers increased significantly post-pandemic, while international acts declined. I later found out this was due to changes in international exchange programs and festival operations, after the pandemic and Brexit. These differences also helped highlight how each festival responded to external challenges.


I focused on making the festival data section feel like a professional piece of writing that could sit comfortably in our industry-facing report. I avoided over-interpreting the numbers and concentrated on what the data clearly showed and could be visually supported. Once I had a strong structure in place and was close to finalising the draft, I circled back to the introduction. I made sure it clearly set up what the reader could expect and gave enough context to keep them engaged.

There wasn’t any redrafting needed between me and Owen at this stage. We’d split the section clearly, had a good foundation to build on, and our writing styles worked well side by side. The final piece blended smoothly, and I felt confident that my contributions added value to the report by grounding the internationalisation section in real, evidence-based insight.

Industry Learning and Contextual Influence


In this entry, I explain how my dissertation research on Brexit’s impact on UK touring informed my understanding of international mobility and shaped the way I framed the festival data. Drawing on industry sources and current commentary, I aimed to connect our findings to broader sector trends, ensuring the analysis reflected real-world challenges and shifts in the UK live music landscape. - Summary powered by AI

As my dissertation focused on the impact of Brexit on UK artists touring the EU, I was already immersed in current research around international mobility, visas, and the broader implications for the live music sector. Constantly reading industry reports, case studies, and policy commentary fed directly into how I framed my contribution. This reading meant I wasn’t just focused on the data, I was thinking about what’s changed, why it might be changing, and how that reflects the wider shifts in the UK live music landscape.

I regularly reviewed articles from sources like UK Music, the Musicians’ Union, and LIVE, and kept a close eye on commentary around Brexit, the pandemic, and the changing structure of international touring through both projects. This reading influenced how I approached my contribution, both in tone and in the way I framed the trends in the data.

This wider context also directly shaped how I framed parts of the section. I wasn’t trying to overstate anything, but I did want to reflect what was happening across the industry and how it was affecting local festivals. This was an industry report not a news article. Lines like the one below came out of this thinking, combining the data with a view of the bigger picture:

“The evolving lineups of 2000 Trees Festival and Cheltenham Jazz Festival reflect broader shifts in the UK live music industry and highlights how festivals have adapted to external pressures, audience trends, and industry challenges.”

It wasn’t just about reporting totals; it was about making sure the writing reflected what people across the sector were already talking about.

Delivering the Section: Contribution, Completion and Reflections


In this entry, I reflect on leading the internationalisation section—collecting and analysing festival data, co-developing the survey, and ensuring consistency with Owen. Despite initial group drop-off, I took ownership where needed and helped deliver a clear, evidence-led section, while the wider group maintained strong collaboration across the project’s final delivery. - Summary powered by AI

My focus throughout the project was on delivering the internationalisation section and contributing to group discussions on the direction, content and design. I took the lead on collecting, cleaning, and categorising the festival data, shaping the structure of the section, and writing the analysis. I also worked with Owen to develop the survey questions and to ensure the tone and layout were consistent across the full section. We split the workload clearly, and our ability to collaborate effectively helped make this an efficient and straightforward process.

That said, the initial group originally assigned to our section dropped off early, and in the end, it was essentially just me and Owen who delivered it. The early Jazz Festival data wasn’t collected consistently with our agreed system and was incomplete, so I made the decision to start again. It made more sense to rebuild it properly than try to patch what was there, and it felt like the right call given the importance of maintaining reliable data for the section.

While our section’s small group lost wider collaborative momentum, the general project group remained strong throughout. The collaboration around design, formatting, and presentation was effective. The group stayed engaged and contributed consistently across the project, and there was a shared sense of accountability that kept the wider report moving forward. I think the group found the benefit towards the end with how well everything came together.

I’m proud of what the group delivered, and of my contributions. The section is clean, evidence-led, and adds real value to the report. Despite some of the challenges around group engagement, the experience reinforced how I work best; where needed I can take ownership and lead, support others’ work, help keep things structured, and offer some out-the-box thinking.
