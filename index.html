Festival Data: Defining Categories and Structuring the Process


In this entry, I explain how we structured the process for collecting festival data to complement audience insights in the internationalisation section. I helped define clear categories for local, national, and international artists, ensuring consistent data collection across events, and contributed to decisions that focused on where artists were based and working rather than just their origin. - Summary powered by AI

As we finalised the survey and began shaping the interviews, we planned how we’d collect and organise festival data. We knew the internationalisation section needed to go beyond audience opinion, we also needed to show how festivals were actually booking artists. The challenge was to create a data set that could meaningfully compare local, national, and international artist presence across different events and years.

We began by outlining how to collect the data accurately. After checking in with Andrew for Cheltenham Jazz Festival, we used their official brochures and the Around Town guide to make sure we were capturing both the ticketed artists and those playing free events on the different stages around the town. For 2000 Trees, I relied on ClashFinder, which had been promoted by the festival as the best way to plan your weekend across the years.

Before collecting any names, we agreed on how we would define local, national, and international artists. I suggested a two-stage process, first collect the line-ups, then research where each artist is based. Artists based in Gloucestershire were classed as local. Artists based in the UK but outside Gloucestershire were national. Artists based outside the UK and flying in for the event were international. There were edge cases we needed to consider and prepare for which I found we did well. For example, Mimi Barks, originally from Berlin but now based in London, was classed as national. We made a clear decision that being “international” meant being currently based outside the UK and travelling into the country as a deciding factor. The point was to focus on where the artist was currently working and how available they were to a UK audience, not just where they were born.

As the project progressed, it became clear that a significant amount of the group’s Cheltenham Jazz Festival data had been collected inconsistently with our guidelines. Artists had been recorded multiple times, some weren’t artists at all, and the national/international categorisations hadn’t been applied properly. I couldn’t pinpoint exactly what was wrong, but there were enough inconsistencies across entries that I decided to restart the Jazz Festival data process from scratch to make sure what we included in the report was accurate and usable.

Owen had understandably shifted his focus to the interview section, so I took on rebuilding the Jazz Festival dataset and collecting the 2000 Trees data. Owen took the lead on the survey data analysis and interviews, with some input and feedback from me to balance the workload. While the situation wasn’t ideal, I was okay with adapting my role. I was already invested in the structure, and I wanted to make sure the section delivered something solid which it couldn’t do with wildly incorrect data.

I also changed the original method we set out to use for collecting 2000 Trees festival lineups, having set out to use tour posters. This is a great example of how my thinking evolved across the project. Not just focusing on getting a list of festival lineups, I wanted to accurately collect the data to establish a solid foundation for the analysis. Another example of this evolution was once I’d collected the core data, I thought about cross-checking it, and decided to check the festival’s social media posts to catch any late changes or updates. This did impact the results as several artists withdrew at the last minute. While confirming artist locations, I also checked their social media activity around the time of the festival. This helped me rule out two or three artists who had cancelled just before their set. However, most cancelled artists were replaced, with the overall totals for national and international artists likely balancing out.

1. Role Selection

In this entry, I explain my decision to assist the stage manager on the Big Top stage at Cheltenham Jazz Festival 2025, choosing a hands-on, team-focused role over more management-oriented options. My aim was to engage directly with the core operations of a major performance space, gain practical experience, and observe professional setups in a high-profile environment.

As part of the Production Company module, we were given the opportunity to choose from various roles at Cheltenham Jazz Festival 2025. These were introduced and explained in lectures. We could choose from roles that were either centred on site operations at stages around Cheltenham, or more crew-based roles tied to specific stages on the main site. While some students gravitated toward roles that offered leadership or management experience, I opted for something more embedded by assisting the stage manager on the Big Top stage, the festival’s main performance space.

I didn’t go into this with a clear rationale or a strong ambition to work the biggest stage. It wasn’t about chasing prestige either. The smaller venues didn’t really appeal to me, and I felt that others might benefit more from the experience of the more management-focused roles, given my background. For me, the Big Top offered the chance to be hands-on and contribute in a space where I could still experience the core principles of teamwork. It also had potential for some informal networking, the chance to see some big names perform, and to observe experienced professional setups in action.

4.1 Working the Big Top: Day-to-Day Role

In this entry, I explain my role assisting the Big Top stage manager, which involved supporting stage transitions, maintaining safety, and coordinating with touring crews. The work followed a steady rhythm of downtime and high-intensity bursts, and I quickly adapted to the routine and expectations under James’s clear direction.

The role was to assist the Big Top stage manager, James, with whatever was needed before, during, and after performances. This usually meant staying close by, following his direction, and checking in with the artists’ touring crew to see if they needed any extra support, though most of the time they had everything covered. The core of the job was to help keep things moving on and off stage, and to make sure the performance area stayed clear and safe between sets.

The pace was as expected: long periods of waiting around followed by bursts of high-energy work, usually during changeovers or load-ins. James was clear and direct, so I quickly got used to how he ran the stage area. From there, it was mostly about repeating the same core processes across the week. Having a quieter start to the week gave us time to familiarise ourselves with the role, the equipment, and the general processes to follow for when changeover times became tighter.

4.2 Core Tasks & Responsibilities

In this entry, I detail the practical tasks I carried out to support the Big Top stage manager, from moving equipment and setting up staging to helping artists on and off stage safely. While some duties varied, the core aim remained consistent: keep the stage area safe, clear, and running smoothly throughout the festival.

While some tasks varied slightly depending on the artist or performance setup, the overall focus was always the same: support the stage manager and contribute to keeping the Big Top running smoothly, safely, and on time. This included helping with load-ins and load-outs, moving cases and equipment between trucks and the stage, and rotating platforms into position, guided by either James or a member of the artist’s touring crew. I also assisted with building and breaking down staging platforms to specific sizes. This was generally a case of adding legs, connecting units, and clearing them once no longer needed.



I also helped artists get on and off stage quickly and safely. This sometimes meant marking hazards like loose cables and platform edges or opening the stage curtain. I occasionally collected water and towels from artist liaison ahead of performances and made sure they were cleared quickly afterwards, as water and electrics don’t mix.

There were a few moments where I assisted with backline or drum kit setup. With limited experience in that area, I mostly took on straightforward tasks like lifting items or folding hardware. Outside of these more active moments, one of the main expectations was simply to keep the stage area clear and to be ready to act when needed.

5.1 Evaluating My Contribution

In this section, I reflect on how I approached the role with a focus on consistency, awareness, and knowing when and how to contribute without needing direction at every step. I wasn’t there to lead or take initiative in a visible way, but to quietly support the team, adapt to the workflow, and help maintain a smooth-running stage by being present, reliable, and responsive to the needs of the production.

From the start, I was aware that my role wasn’t about taking the lead or trying to innovate; it was about showing up, fitting into an existing system, and supporting the processes in place. With that in mind, my contribution came through consistency, judgement, and being able to act without needing things explained to me at every turn. That said, the nature of the role still required constant communication with James and the wider teams.

Because of my past experiences, I didn’t require much onboarding. I understood how a changeover flows, how backline is moved safely and efficiently, and when the most helpful thing is simply to stay out of the way. I didn’t need to be told where to stand or when to step back, I could read the room and make those decisions. This kind of awareness made me useful, even when I wasn’t doing anything hands-on.

I also tried to manage my time around the needs of the stage, rather than just sticking rigidly to the schedule provided. On the first day I worked with James and the production office to adjust my shifts so that I was present when there was a load-in, a changeover, or a build. This meant adjusting start and finish times to better align with the stage schedule and downtime. That flexibility came from understanding how these systems operate and recognising that it’s about being present when it counts.

Throughout the week, I followed instruction without needing things repeated, asked questions when something was unclear, and quietly looked for ways to be useful even if it meant just making space and keeping the area clear for others to work. I wasn’t trying to stand out or take over. My aim was to do the job well, support the people who needed supporting, and keep things moving without adding any unnecessary noise.

5.2 Evaluating the Experience

In this section, I reflect on how the placement reinforced rather than introduced key professional skills I’ve developed over time. While the experience didn’t offer new learning in the traditional sense, it validated my existing knowledge of live event workflows, communication, and team dynamics. I was reminded of the importance of preparation and clear protocols, especially when unexpected issues arise, and gained a few small pieces of industry-specific knowledge that added to my practical vocabulary.

What stood out the most across the week was how different my experience of the placement felt compared to others from the course. For some, it was among their first experiences of working with a professional crew, the first time seeing how artists are moved on and off stage, or how small adjustments can make a big difference during a changeover. This wasn’t down to a lack of engagement, or an issue with the placement itself, as the structure and support were strong. It was simply because I’d already seen a lot of these processes elsewhere.

In previous roles, I’ve worked within live venues, built and broken down amusement rides, and worked the main stage at Black Deer Festival. In my current position at Northern Music Co. I support our roster of international touring artists with tour planning and prep, from production planning to crew coordination. Therefore, this experience didn’t present anything new to me, but it did allow me to return to familiar processes in a live, professional setting.

What I took from the experience wasn’t so much growth as it was confirmation. It reminded me that I know how to carry myself within a team and in a production setting. I know how to stay useful without getting in the way, how to communicate and support other teams without overstepping, and how to follow instruction while still using my own initiative. These are things built through repetition, and I’ve been fortunate to come into this experience having refined those core skills over many years.

There were some small things I picked up. I learnt that “wedges” referred to stage monitors and that “peli case” isn’t a particular item; it’s just a very good brand of case.

This was also a reminder of how unpredictable live environments can be, something that became especially clear during a power cut that interrupted a headline performance. While these moments are part of live production, it also highlighted a gap: there didn’t seem like there was a clear show stop procedure or agreement in place, which created confusion across the board about how to respond. It was a reminder of how important advance planning, communication, and agreed protocols are, even in well-run setups like this.

5.3 Considering Future Directions

This part of the experience prompted me to think more critically about the relationship between technical and managerial roles on tour. Observing the separation between logistical oversight and hands-on technical knowledge made me reflect on my own skill set and career direction. With my existing experience supporting international touring artists and coordinating production logistics through NMC, I’ve begun to see tour management as a realistic and fitting progression—one where I could bridge both worlds and bring added value through a combined understanding of planning and production.

I noticed that many tour managers working on these larger setups don’t always have much hands-on knowledge of the equipment or infrastructure they travel with. I was surprised by how little some knew which is not a criticism, just something I hadn’t quite expected. It's made me consider whether that’s a direction I might want to explore further. Given that I already work closely with international artists, venues, and production teams through NMC, the idea of moving into a tour management role, with a solid logistical base and some technical understanding to build on, feels like a natural next step for me that's worth serious consideration.
